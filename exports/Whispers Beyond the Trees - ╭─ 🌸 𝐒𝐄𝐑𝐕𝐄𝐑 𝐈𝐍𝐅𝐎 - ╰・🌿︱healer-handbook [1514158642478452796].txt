[🌱] WBT Custom Herb Guide
A Note on Realism: While we use real plants and their properties for inspiration, wolves can't perform complex surgery, set complex broken bones, or cure every ailment. This guide is designed to enhance storytelling, offering a grounded and believable framework for the struggles and triumphs of a pack's Healer.

Plants of the river are often associated with water, emotion, and purification, reflecting the river's flow and adaptability.

**Meadowsweet (Filipendula ulmaria)**

**Effect**: A potent pain reliever and fever reducer. Chewing the flowers or leaves can alleviate headaches, rheumatism, and soothe digestive upset. Its numbing quality makes it ideal for treating minor mouth and tooth pain. 

**Where to Find**: Found on damp grasslands, waterlogged meadows, and along fluctuating river banks. Look for its tall, frothy sprays of creamy-white flowers with a fragrant, almond-like scent. 

**Growing Conditions**: Prefers full sun to partial shade and consistently moist soil. It is a perennial, dying back in winter and re-growing from a low rosette each spring. 

**Alder Bark (Alnus spp.)**
**Effect**: The bark of the alder tree is a specific remedy for toothaches and sore gums. A wolf can chew on a small twig to release the sap, which helps numb the pain and reduce inflammation. 

**Where to Find**: Alder trees are almost always found in wet, low-lying areas, thriving on the banks of rivers, streams, and swamps. Their bark is smooth and greyish-brown.

**Growing Conditions**: Thrives in wet, waterlogged soil and is often one of the first trees to colonize a riverbank after a flood.

**Douglas' Sagewort (Artemisia douglasiana)**
**Effect**: The aromatic foliage of this plant has powerful ceremonial and medicinal uses. It can be chewed and applied to wounds to prevent infection or used in cleansing rituals. A healer may also use it to calm a wolf's nerves before a stressful hunt or journey.
 
**Where to Find**: Prefers to grow along streambanks, floodplains, and open, damp woodland edges. Its leaves are deeply lobed and silvery-green.

**Growing Conditions**: Grows best in full sun with moist, well-drained soil. It is a hardy perennial that can form dense, fragrant colonies.

{Embed}
**Cattail (Typha latifolia)**

**Effect**: A wolf's most versatile plant, often called the "supermarket of the swamp." The rootstocks can be chewed into a poultice for wounds, burns, and stings. The gelatinous substance between young leaves stops bleeding and acts as a mild anesthetic for toothaches. Pollen from the flower head can be used as a dry dressing for wounds and helps soothe respiratory issues. Some packs believe cattail pollen carries blessings of abundance.

**Where to Find:** Grows in dense stands along riverbanks, pond edges, and marshes. Easily recognized by its brown, sausage-shaped flower heads atop tall stalks.

**Growing Conditions**: Thrives in shallow, slow-moving fresh water or wet, muddy soil in full sun. Forms extensive colonies through spreading rhizomes.

**Purple Loosestrife (Lythrum salicaria)**

**Effect**: A powerful astringent that helps stop bleeding from deep wounds. Chewed leaves can be applied to torn flesh to slow blood flow and promote clotting. Some healers use it in tea form to treat diarrhea and stomach complaints. Due to its aggressive nature in wetlands, packs may have legends about it spreading from a single drop of blood spilled in anger.

**Where to Find**: Prefers wet meadows, river margins, and marshy areas. Its distinctive tall spikes of magenta-purple flowers bloom from early to late summer.

**Growing Conditions**: Thrives in full sun with consistently moist to wet soil. Can tolerate some standing water.

The mysterious swamp offers plants of great potency, often tied to magic, curses, and powerful transformations.

** Swamp Milkweed (Asclepias incarnata)**

**Effect**: In folk magic, a decoction (a concentrated tea) of this plant was used to grant a person the strength to physically subdue a witch. For a wolf Herbalist/Healer, this herb could be used in rituals to temporarily grant a pack member immense strength to break a magical curse or overcome a dark spirit. Its medicinal use is for soothing rheumatic pains.

 **Where to Find**: Look for its vibrant clusters of pink flowers in swamps, ditches, and other very wet, marshy areas. 

 **Growing Conditions**: Requires full sun and consistently wet, boggy soil. It is a vital host plant for monarch butterflies.

**Lizard's Tail (Saururus cernuus)**

**Effect**: Also known as swamp root, this plant's mashed roots can be used as a poultice to soothe inflammation of the breast and back. It is also known to help reduce fevers and body aches. When crushed, its leaves have a citrusy or sassafras-like scent.

**Where to Find**: This semi-aquatic perennial grows in shallow, still water and wet mud along the edges of swamps and marshes. Its most distinctive feature is its long, drooping spike of tiny white flowers that turn brown, resembling a lizard's tail. 

**Growing Conditions**: An obligate wetland plant, it needs saturated, waterlogged soils or shallow water to survive. 

** Skunk Cabbage (Symplocarpus foetidus)**

**Effect**: A powerful medicinal plant used to treat respiratory diseases like coughs and asthma, as well as nervous disorders and rheumatism. It must be used with great care, as it is toxic if not properly prepared (e.g., dried). A healer would need to have extensive knowledge to use it safely.

**Where to Find**: One of the first plants to emerge in very early spring, even through snow. It is found in wet, swampy woodlands and along streams.

**Growing Conditions**: Thrives in waterlogged, mucky soils in shaded areas. It's famous for generating its own heat to melt through snow and ice.

**Boneset (Eupatorium perfoliatum)**

**Effect:** True to its name, boneset is a specific remedy for fevers accompanied by deep bone pain and body aches that make a wolf feel as though their bones are breaking. It induces sweating to break stubborn fevers and helps relieve severe coughs. A healer would use it when a pack member is trembling with cold despite burning with fever.

**Where to Find**: Grows abundantly in low-lying, wet ground, swamps, marshes, and along stream banks. Its clusters of small white flowers are distinctive.

**Growing Conditions**: Prefers full sun to partial shade with consistently wet, rich soil. The plant's leaves appear to be pierced by the stem, giving it its scientific name.

**Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis)**

**Effect**: A potent immune booster and fever reducer. The bark, leaves, and roots are toxic, but the flowers and ripe berries, when prepared correctly, create powerful medicine for colds, flu, and coughs. Elderberry syrup soothes sore throats and builds a wolf's resistance to sickness. In swamp folklore, elder is the "witch's tree" and offers protection against evil magic when planted near a den.

**Where to Find**: Grows as a shrub in wet, swampy woodlands, along fencerows, and near stream banks. Clusters of tiny white flowers appear in early summer, followed by dark purple berries.

**Growing Conditions**: Thrives in moist, well-drained soil but tolerates occasional flooding. Prefers full sun to partial shade.

The harsh, high-altitude environment produces resilient and powerful herbs, often associated with protection, resilience, and spiritual strength.

 **Wolfsbane / Monkshood (Aconitum napellus)**

**Effect**: A plant of potent magic and deadly poison. It is one of the few substances that can ward off or cure lycanthropy. In your roleplay, it could be the only known cure for a wolf bitten by a rogue werewolf. However, it is so dangerous that a single mistake in dosage could be fatal. It can also be used in tiny, diluted amounts as a powerful painkiller.

**Where to Find**: Thrives in moist, nutrient-rich mountain meadows and along cool, shaded stream banks at higher elevations.

**Growing Conditions**: Prefers cool summers, rich, consistently moist soil, and partial shade. All parts of the plant are highly toxic.

**Edelweiss (Leontopodium nivale)**

**Effect**: A symbol of rugged beauty, dedication, and purity. Its primary effect is as a remedy for abdominal and respiratory diseases, earning it the nickname "bellyache flower." 

**Where to Find**: Grows in remote, rocky, limestone mountain areas at high altitudes (around 1,800 meters / 5,900 feet).

**Growing Conditions**: Requires poor, well-drained, rocky soil, full sun, and cool temperatures.

**Mountain Ash / Rowan (Sorbus aucuparia)**

**Effect**: While a tree rather than a simple herb, its wood and berries have immense protective powers. Rowan is believed to ward off evil spirits, dark magic, and even werewolves, who cannot cross a barrier made of its wood. For a wolf pack, placing a branch of rowan around the camp could create a barrier against hostile spirits or magic.

**Where to Find**: A small, hardy tree found throughout mountain forests and rocky slopes. It is easily recognized by its clusters of bright red berries in autumn.

**Growing Conditions**: Very adaptable, thriving in poor soil but requiring good drainage. It is extremely cold-hardy.

**Arnica (Arnica montana)**

**Effect**: A specific remedy for bruises, sprains, and muscle soreness. A poultice made from arnica flowers reduces swelling and speeds recovery from physical trauma. It is so valued that mountain packs may mark arnica patches as shared healing grounds. Must not be ingested—external use only, as internal use causes vomiting and organ damage.

**Where to Find**: Grows in subalpine mountain meadows, open woodlands, and rocky slopes. Its bright yellow, daisy-like flowers bloom in summer.

**Growing Conditions**: Requires full sun, acidic soil, and cool mountain climates. Does not tolerate heat or humidity.


** Prickly Ash (Zanthoxylum americanum)**

**Effect**: Also called the "toothache tree." Chewing its bark or berries produces a numbing, tingling sensation in the mouth, making it valuable for dental pain. It also improves circulation and warms cold extremities: a wolf caught in a mountain snowstorm might be given prickly ash to restore blood flow to frozen paws or tail.

**Where to Find**: Grows as a shrub in rocky woodlands, along mountain slopes, and near streams. Its branches are covered in sharp, paired thorns, and its berries have a citrus-like scent.

**Growing Conditions**: Prefers full sun to partial shade with well-drained, somewhat dry soil. Tolerates poor, rocky ground.

 **Coneflower / Echinacea (Echinacea angustifolia)**

Effect: A premier immune stimulant that helps a wolf's body fight off bacterial and viral infections. It is most effective when used at the very first sign of illness: a sneeze, a cough, a wound beginning to redden. Applied externally to wounds, it prevents infection. In mountain packs, coneflower is often part of a healer's "first response" kit.

Where to Find: Prefers dry, open prairies, rocky hillsides, and mountain meadows. Its distinctive purple, drooping petals surround a spiky, cone-shaped center.

Growing Conditions: Thrives in full sun with well-drained, dry to medium-moist soil. Extremely drought-tolerant once established.

Plantain (Plantago major)

Effect: A ubiquitous healing herb that every wolf should know. The leaves, chewed into a pulp, draw out splinters, thorns, and infection from wounds. It stops bleeding, soothes insect stings and snake bites, and can be eaten to settle an upset stomach. Plantain is the "healer's helper"—common, reliable, and effective.

**Foxglove (Digitalis purpurea)**

**Effect**: Contains cardiac glycosides that disrupt the heart's rhythm. Even a small amount causes vomiting, irregular heartbeat, and eventually death. Some corrupted healers might use foxglove as a poison disguised as "heart medicine." Its flowers are beautiful, but its touch can be fatal.

**Where to Find**: Thrives in forest clearings, along mountain woodland edges, and in disturbed soil near old wolf trails.

**Identifying Features**: Tall spikes of drooping, bell-shaped purple or white flowers with spots inside the blossom.

**Deadly Nightshade / Belladonna (Atropa belladonna)**

**Effect**: A plant associated with dark curses and assassination. All parts are toxic, even if the sweet berries tempt unwary pups. Ingestion causes hallucinations, delirium, paralysis, and death. In wolf folklore, nightshade might be used in rituals to commune with dark spirits or curse an enemy pack. Touching it can cause skin irritation.

**Where to Find**: Grows in shaded, moist areas of forests, near limestone outcrops, and along old walls or ruins.

**Identifying Features**: Purple-brown, bell-shaped flowers followed by shiny black berries about the size of a wolf's claw. Leaves have an unpleasant, sticky smell when crushed.

Trees
Trees offer year-round medicine when the ground is frozen and herbs are scarce.

**Willow (Salix spp.)**

**Effect**: Nature's original pain reliever. The inner bark contains salicin, the precursor to modern aspirin. Chewing a willow twig eases headaches, reduces fever, and alleviates the ache of old injuries. In winter, when other herbs are buried under snow, willow bark becomes a healer's most reliable tool.

**Where to Find**: Almost always near water: riverbanks, lake shores, swamp edges. Easily identified by its long, slender leaves and deeply furrowed bark.

**Growing Conditions**: Thrives in consistently moist to wet soil in full sun.

**Oak (Quercus spp.)**

**Effect**: An astringent powerhouse. The inner bark and young leaves stop bleeding and dry up infected wounds. Chewing oak bark tightens tissue and slows blood flow from deep cuts. Gargling an oak infusion soothes sore throats. Oak is also a symbol of strength and endurance—carrying an acorn might be a young healer's first lesson in protective magic.

**Where to Find**: Common in forests of all types but prefers well-drained soil. Oak leaves are lobed, and its bark is deeply ridged.

**Growing Conditions**: Prefers full sun and well-drained soil. Very long-lived trees that may shelter wolf dens beneath their roots.

**Pine (Pinus spp.)**

**Effect**: The ultimate cold-weather medicine. Pine needle tea is rich in vitamin C and treats respiratory infections—a wolf coughing in winter might be given pine-infused snowmelt. Pine resin acts as a natural antiseptic and can be applied to wounds to seal them against infection. Pine pitch mixed with crushed herbs creates a waterproof bandage for deep wounds.

**Where to Find**: Grows in forests of all elevations, often dominating mountain slopes. Easily identified by its needle-like leaves, cones, and distinctive scent.

**Growing Conditions**: Highly adaptable but prefers well-drained soil and full sun.

**Slippery Elm (Ulmus rubra)**

**Effect**: A gentle but powerful soothing herb. The inner bark, when mixed with water, becomes a slick, mucilaginous gel that coats and soothes sore throats, coughs, and digestive inflammation. A starving wolf can eat slippery elm bark to settle a cramped, empty stomach. It also draws out infection from wounds when used as a poultice.

**Where to Find**: Grows in rich, moist soils of forests and along streams. Its bark is reddish-brown with flattened ridges.

**Growing Conditions**: Prefers partial shade with moist, well-drained soil.

> - Freshly gathered herbs should always be dried before longterm storage. Spread them out in a dry, shaded but open place. This prevents moisture from building up, which can eventually cause rot. Though avoid placing most herbs in direct sunlight for long periods, as strong heat can dry them too quickly and reduce the effectiveness. Only do this if the herbs are damp or wet to remove excess moisture before being moved to shade.
> 
> - Different types of herbs should always be stored separately. Roots, leaves, flowers, berries, and seeds should each have their own space. Some herbs can affect others if mixed and cause them to lose strength or take on unwanted scents or effects. Keeping them apart helps to preserve their quality and makes them easier to find when needed.
> 
> - Herbs should be stored in a cool, dry, and secure area. Bracken, moss, or woven grass is commonly used to create small nests or pockets for each herb to be collected in. These storage spaces also protect herbs from dirt and many pests.
> 
> - Herb stores must be checked regularly. Any herbs that are wilted, moldy, brittle beyond use, or rotting should be removed immediately. Using spoiled herbs can do more harm than good. This also helps to keep track of which herbs are running low and need to be gathered again.
> 
> - During leafbare (winter), herbs must be stored more carefully, as cold and damp conditions can damage the supplies easier. Extra insulation, such as thicker moss lining is needed if snow falls as melting snow can cause herbs to rot. Warmer seasons is usually the time where most herbs will be collected and stored.
> 
> - Even after drying, herbs should not be kept in direct light for long periods. Too much light can weaken their strength and cause them to lose their scent and effectiveness.

- Always prepare ahead, gather and store generously during greenleaf when herbs are plentiful.

### Treatment Plans
1. Shallow Cuts / Scrapes
Symptoms: Broken skin, light bleeding, no deep tissue exposed.

Primary Herbs: Yarrow, plantain, marigold.

Alternatives (by territory):

River: Meadowsweet (also numbs pain)

Forest: Oak leaf poultice

Preparation: Chew leaves into a moist poultice.

Treatment:

Lick wound clean or dab with damp moss.
Apply thin layer of poultice.
If bleeding persists, add cobwebs.
Cover with a leaf or more cobwebs.
Reapply twice daily.

Primary Herbs: Goldenrod, oak leaves, horsetail, comfrey, purple loosestrife (stops bleeding fast).

Pack with goldenrod or oak leaf poultice.
Press cobwebs firmly on top.
Bind with rush or bindweed vines.
Change dressing twice daily.

### Healing Techniques
Checking the spine: Gently nipping or pressing along a wolf’s spine, tail, or legs to feel for breaks or misalignments.

Licking fur: Cleans wounds, calms a wolf in shock or grief, and warms an extremely cold wolf. Newborn pups are licked vigorously to stimulate breathing and warmth.

Herbs for the dead: Mint, lavender, or rosemary placed on the body before burial to hide death‑scent from predators and spirits.

Masking bitter taste: Honey, sweet berries, or fresh blood from prey can be used to make herbs easier to swallow.

Cold water therapy: Holding a swollen wound or sore pad in cold stream water soothes swelling and pain.

Moss as a tool: Soaked moss provides water for feverish or weak wolves.

Clearing the chest: Encouraging a coughing wolf to howl or bark deeply can help loosen mucus from the lungs.

Checking pregnancy or bloat: Pressing an ear to a wolf’s belly while gently patting – a hard belly with distant rushing sounds suggests pregnancy; a dull, painful give suggests worms; bubbly sounds suggest bloating.

Swimming therapy: Excellent for wolves with weak muscles, healing bones, or elders with inflamed joints.

Removing burrs or torn claws: Pull at an angle, not straight up, to reduce pain.

Drying herbs: Never harvest just after rain or in heavy morning dew – wet herbs rot. Leave damp herbs in the sun to dry.

Sustainable harvesting: Never completely clear a herb patch unless truly desperate and certain another supply exists.

Poison berries (holly, yew, nightshade): Can be used to kill predators or, only if the wolf asks, to end unbearable suffering when no cure remains.

Gathering trips: Plan when two or three herb stores are low; don’t leave camp too often.

### Common Illnesses & Injuries
Pupcough – Affects weak pups, especially those with difficult births or slow start of breathing. Harsh cough that weakens but is usually harmless; can lead to weak lungs if untreated.

Whitecough – Like a cold: runny nose and eyes, slight cough. If untreated, can become Greencough.

Greencough – Like flu: runny nose/eyes, chest pain, trouble breathing, wet/mucousy cough, tiredness, sometimes high fever. Can be fatal if untreated.

Blackcough – Rare, almost always fatal. No known cure. Begins with chills, cough, lack of appetite; then weakness, dizziness, nausea; cough worsens until blackish blood is hacked up. Wheezing and difficulty breathing are what usually kill. Believed that coughed‑up blood can spread the disease.

Shock (Emotional) – After severe trauma: lack of emotion, denial, ceasing to communicate, rocking. Treat with poppy seeds, comfort, and in extreme cases, valerian.

Shock (Physical) – From blood loss or extreme pain: chills, numbness, rapid heartbeat, weakness. Life‑threatening. Treat with water, honey, and small quantities of dry yarrow. Stop bleeding and treat injuries.

Smoke Inhalation – Caused by breathing smoke from wildfires or den fires. Severity judged by coughing, wheezing, nausea, and listening to the chest.

Cloudmouth (Rabies) – Highly contagious via bite, scratch, or saliva. Symptoms appear 2 weeks to a year after exposure: fear of water, inability to swallow, sleeplessness, mania, foaming at the mouth, paranoia, rage. Always fatal within days. No treatment. Infected wolves must be heavily sedated and left to die away from pack territory. If the wolf still has sanity, they may be offered deathberries as a mercy.
