"Mud."

"When the shaking-sickness took the pups, I sat in mud for three days. It filled my fur, my ears, the gaps where my teeth are loose. It stopped the thinking. Mud doesn't judge. Mud doesn't remember names. It just . . . holds. Like a hug but better."

He pulls his paw out. A leech dangles from his wrist. He watches it swell.

"Moss is too soft. Makes me think of fur, living fur, warm fur, fur that will rot. Stone is cold and it lies; pretends to be still while the Maw's belly churns underneath. Water moves too fast. Always leaving."

He flicks the leech off. It lands in the pool with a small plop.

"But mud? Mud stays. Mud is the Maw's tongue. When I press my face into it, like this—"

He lowers his head, presses his scarred muzzle into the bank, and holds there for five, ten, fifteen heartbeats. When he lifts it, his pale eyes are wetter than usual, and a thin film of black silt coats his left eye.

"—I can feel it chewing. Slow. Patient. And for a little while, I chew back."

He smiles. His teeth are stained brown.

"So yes. Mud. Do you want some? You look anxious. I can find you a good patch. The kind with the worms."
