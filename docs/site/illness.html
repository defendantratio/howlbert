illness & herbs

the maw chews. illness, injury, birth, and death are all the maw eating.

illness in howlbert blends real canid ecology with the maw faith. over thirty diseases, injuries, and genetic conditions, each with daily penalties to hunger, hydration, hp, hunt yield, or pain exhaustion. every sickness has a wolf-world name; the packs do not know human words for the things that eat them.

the packs count a wounded mind as real as a wounded leg. each of these is a tracked condition with its own penalties and its own calming herbs.

the green tongue

over a hundred herbs with real properties, gathered by foragers, kept in the healers' den store, and worked by medics into poultices, teas, salves, and surgeries. some herbs heal; some are poison held one leaf from mercy. wolves cannot perform miracles; they set what bones they can, stitch what they can reach, and lose the rest to the maw.

the healer's code: medics are not blocked from courtship, mating, or pups, but the den punishes discovery hard. a caught court costs standing and exiles existing pups. a discovered birth or adoption is always public and exiles the litter and the healer. medics keep a neutral scent so they can treat any wolf; crossing that line is treason. — the green tongue's oldest law

full herb compendium

all known herbs, their effects, what they cure, their side effects, and how to prepare them. this is the complete reference for the green tongue. wolf-world names are used; real names are given in brackets where helpful. preparation methods: dried, poultice, juice, eaten raw, tea, gargle, sweetened, ointment, sap, rub, eaten cooked, simmered milk.

adder's tongue common reroll failed poison save with advantage if within 1 minute of sting; juice treats swollen eyes and cleans deep gashes.
cures: sting-swell (mild poison), swollen eye, deep gash, shaking-sickness (seizure disorder)
side effects: can cause swelling, blistering, itching; toxic in large amounts; nausea, vomiting, diarrhea.
preparation: fresh roots or leaves simmered in milk; juice extracted from leaves.
alder bark uncommon mistmoor prevents infection and reduces swelling.
cures: root-rot (tooth infection), infected wound, gut-run (diarrhea)
side effects: fresh bark causes severe vomiting; prolonged use may cause low potassium, heart problems.
preparation: bark applied as poultice; tea used as mouthwash.
arnica rare greyspire halves bruise and sprain recovery (external only); anti-inflammatory poultice for joint pain. never eat; internal use is lethal.
cures: sprained leg, punctured paw, joint-rot (arthritis)
side effects: highly toxic if ingested; nausea, vomiting, irregular heartbeat, coma; topical may cause itching or rash.
preparation: external use only as poultice or ointment.
beech leaves common carry herbs; nut oil prevents infection and clears minor surface wounds. leaf tea is mildly antiseptic and eases leaf-bare cough (cold/chill).
cures: leaf-bare cough (cold/chill), infected wound
side effects: excessive intake may be toxic; unripe nuts cause vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain.
preparation: leaves used in tea; ointments for burns, sores, and ulcers.
bindweed vines common relieves gut complaints and urinary problems; immune-stimulating tonic for fever. tough vines lash splints to broken bones, speeding supported breaks (bone healing minus 7 days). strong laxative in large amounts; use sparingly.
cures: the refusing (eating distress), water-scorch (urinary infection), shiver-fever (influenza), gut-stone (constipation)
side effects: strong laxative; large amounts cause stomach pain; toxic to livestock.
preparation: leaves may be eaten cooked to reduce oxalic acid; seeds used as purgative.
blackberry (bramble) common soothes insect stings and ends non-magical venom; root bark is strongly astringent; settles diarrhea and gut upsets.
cures: gut-run (diarrhea), the refusing (eating distress), infected wound, sting-swell (mild poison)
side effects: prolonged or high-dose use may cause digestive upset or constipation from tannins.
preparation: leaves chewed into pulp and applied to stings; root bark eaten raw or as tea.
bloodroot restricted toxic 3d6 poison damage (dc 16 half). prolonged contact causes tissue damage and scarring.
cures: none (poison)
side effects: nausea, vomiting, drowsiness, vertigo, contact dermatitis; topical use can cause tissue damage.
preparation: external use only as ointment; extreme caution.
boneset common mistmoor reroll failed disease save with advantage; take the better result. slows early cloudmouth (rabies) (one sunrise, no cure). eases deep respiratory congestion and fever pain. fresh plant is toxic; always dried.
cures: shiver-fever (influenza), leaf-bare cough (cold/chill)
side effects: large amounts cause severe diarrhea; contains hepatotoxic pyrrolizidine alkaloids; liver damage.
preparation: dried leaves and flowers as tea.
borage common extra milk for nursing mother; one additional pup. reduces fever, eases cough, and calms anxiety and low spirits. must be used fresh; drying destroys properties.
cures: shiver-fever (influenza), leaf-bare cough (cold/chill), the skitters (anxiety), the low-spirit (depression)
side effects: upset stomach, headache, nausea; possible liver damage with prolonged use.
preparation: must be used fresh, never dried; leaves and roots eaten raw.
broom common bind broken bones and splint fractures; anti-inflammatory poultice for bruising and sprains; move at half speed without worsening. toxic in large doses; cardiac effects and weakness.
cures: fractured rib, sprained leg, broken jaw
side effects: contains toxic alkaloids; weakness, blurred vision, cardiac arrhythmias, nausea.
preparation: chewed and applied as poultice.
burdock root common poultice draws infection from bites and open wounds after 24h rest. eases skin disease and early growth-sickness (cancer). diuretic; avoid when dehydrated.
cures: infected wound, deep gash, scratch-bare (mange), growth-sickness (cancer)
side effects: generally safe; may cause allergic reactions; high doses may cause liver injury.
preparation: root dug up, washed, chewed into pulp, applied to bites and wounds.
burnet common leaf applied to cuts staunches bleeding; settles gut sickness and deep wounds. 1 leaf per day ignores first exhaustion from a forced march.
cures: sprained leg, gut-run (diarrhea), deep gash
side effects: limited documented side effects.
preparation: applied as poultice; root eaten raw as astringent.
catchweed burrs common burrs hold poultices in place; extends duration 4 hours without harming the pelt. clears urinary complaints.
cures: water-scorch (urinary infection)
side effects: limited documented side effects.
preparation: burrs attached to pelt over poultices.
cattail common silverrush mistmoor pollen is hemostatic; stops bleeding like yarrow; analgesic; mildly antiseptic.
cures: deep gash, sprained leg, infected wound
side effects: generally safe short-term; may cause mild gi discomfort; avoid in pregnancy.
preparation: pollen eaten raw; young shoots and rhizomes eaten cooked.
celandine common removes eye swelling and restores eyesight within 1 hour; treats liver complaints and belly-grind (gallstones); eases leaf-bare cough (cold/chill). large doses cause vomiting; use sparingly.
cures: swollen eye, partial blindness, yellow-eye (hepatitis), leaf-bare cough (cold/chill), belly-grind (gallstones)
side effects: large quantities cause vomiting and purging; fresh juice is local irritant; may cause dry mouth.
preparation: juice trickled into the eye.
chamomile common compound advantage on wisdom saves vs fear for 1 hour. reduces fever, calms nerves, soothes eating troubles, and eases grief. mildly sedative; aids sleep and recovery.
cures: the skitters (anxiety), the sleepless (insomnia), the refusing (eating distress), shiver-fever (influenza), the hollowing (grief), fever-wander (delirium)
side effects: generally safe; allergic reactions possible, especially in ragweed-sensitive wolves.
preparation: leaves and flowers eaten raw; tea made from flowers.
chervil common removes nausea; eases redscratch itch; leaf juice clears infected wounds. stimulates appetite and eases gut troubles in wasting wolves.
cures: infected wound, gut-run (diarrhea), redscratch, the refusing (eating distress), wasting-sickness (chronic wasting), brood-rot (brucellosis, early fever)
side effects: generally safe; potential for mild gi discomfort or allergic reactions.
preparation: juice extracted from leaves or root for infected wounds and bellyache.
chickweed common roadside compound ends green-cough (3 doses per 24 hours); poultice soothes skin conditions and reduces mild swelling. anti-bacterial; promotes wound healing.
cures: green-cough (mild respiratory), leaf-bare cough (cold/chill), infected wound
side effects: high doses may cause vertigo, weakness, headache, difficulty breathing.
preparation: used as tea or poultice.
cobnuts common +1 stealth when approaching prey; rich in protein and fiber; restores +8 hunger on patrol.
cures: none
side effects: may trigger allergic reactions in tree-nut-sensitive individuals.
preparation: eaten raw or cooked.
cobwebs common auto-stabilize dying wolf; bandages deep gashes and burns. required for field dressing. can increase infection risk if wound is already dirty; clean wound first.
cures: dying, deep gash, shaking-sickness (seizure disorder), scorched hide
side effects: can increase risk of infection if wound is dirty.
preparation: gathered in a swath and applied to bleeding wound; wrapped around injury.
coltsfoot common ends green-cough after 1 dose; leaf pulp soothes cracked and sore paw pads. toxic long-term; contains alkaloids that damage the liver; use short-term only.
cures: green-cough (mild respiratory), leaf-bare cough (cold/chill), punctured paw, chestbind (asthma)
side effects: likely unsafe; contains hepatotoxic pyrrolizidine alkaloids that damage liver and lungs.
preparation: leaves chewed to pulp and eaten for shortness of breath.
comfrey uncommon thistlehide poultice heals 1d4 hp on deep wounds; stimulates tissue regeneration and prevents scar tissue.
cures: fractured rib, broken jaw, sprained leg, deep gash

the healing system

howlbert implements a detailed healing system that blends realism with the maw faith. healing is not magic; it is careful, dangerous work.

hp and recovery

hp = 10 + strength + survival (con score). recovery is slow and depends on the wolf's condition, injury load, and access to herbs.

  • long rest – once per sunrise, restores hp. recovery is modified by: active injury load (−1 per injury), hunger below 30 (−1), and winter season (+1).
  • pain exhaustion – a separate tracker (0 to 5) distinct from main exhaustion. accumulates from painful injuries and diseases. herbs like willow bark and poppy target this pool specifically. at 5 stacks, it overflows into main exhaustion.
  • death saves – at 0 hp, a wolf must make death saves unless stabilized. medics can stabilize with a medicine check, often using yarrow or horsetail for a bonus.

herbs and healing

  • rarity – common, uncommon, rare, very rare, or restricted.
  • effect – what the herb does mechanically.
  • cures – which conditions it treats.
  • side effects – mechanical penalties for misuse.
  • preparation – how to use it (poultice, tea, eaten raw, etc.).
  • pack-specific – some herbs are only found in certain territories.
  • poison/restricted – dangerous herbs that only medics may handle.
forage use /field action:forage to forage in your territory or :verge to forage at the thunderpath edge or twoleg compound. roll to find herbs. prepare use /herbs action:prepare to turn raw herbs into usable poultices, teas, or preparations. treat use /medic action:treat to apply a prepared herb to a wound or condition. requires the correct herb and a medicine check. field dressing any wolf can use /medic action:field_dressing (survival dc 14) to pack cobwebs against a deep gash, suppressing the sunrise bleed for one day. wound wash medic action, dc 10 medicine. dock leaves or horsetail flush an infected wound clean (/medic action:wound_wash). stabilize use /medic action:stabilize on a dying wolf. yarrow and horsetail give bonuses.

herb side effects and addiction

the compendium's warnings are mechanical, not flavor. eating an apply-only herb, pressing a skin-toxin into an open wound, or dosing a pregnant wolf backfires (hp loss, miscarriage risk, gut upset). sedatives like poppy, valerian, and willow build tolerance and withdrawal with repeated use.

vomiting: nauseating herbs (adder's tongue, alder bark, celandine, mountain ash, sage, rosemary, saffron, sorrel, elderberry, coltsfoot, skunk cabbage, juniper) eaten internally can be brought back up on a failed constitution save; the wolf retches up the dose and its meal (−8 hunger, −6 hydration, −3 mood). a trained herbalist doses more gently.

cumulative organ damage: the compendium's "long-term use" warnings now bite. repeated internal doses of an organ-straining herb are tallied for life; past the herb's threshold the harm becomes a permanent long-term condition that shows on /vitals: diuretics that waste potassium (alder bark) → The Sapping (−1 str/con); kidney-stressing herbs (juniper, sorrel, wood sorrel) → Bitter-Water (−1 con); liver-toxic herbs (oak bark, ragwort, comfrey) → Yellow-Wither (−1 con and on disease/poison saves); thiamine-leaching herbs (horsetail, rush stalks, stinging nettle) → The Trembles (−1 dex/wis). vary your herbs; do not lean on one draught.

herb tolerance

using the same herb on the same injury within 3 days raises the treatment dc by +2. varied treatment is encouraged by yarrow and plantain also curing torn claw.

bone-set failure

bone injuries (fractured rib, spinal injury, sprained leg, broken jaw) left untreated for 3+ sunrises cause permanent −1 attribute malunion (malunion_X in long-term injuries).

medic overextension

healing 5 or more wolves in one sunrise adds +1 exhaustion on rollover.

healer's instinct

a full medic earns +1 wisdom permanently after 20 successful treatments (healer_instinct long-term trait).

herb rarity

herbs run from common to very rare, with a separate restricted tier for the poisons a medic is trusted to keep.

  • common – found on most forages. the backbone of the green tongue.
  • uncommon – requires knowledge of where to look. often pack-specific.
  • rare – hard to find, even in the right territory. prized by medics.
  • very rare – almost legendary. found only in one place, if at all.
  • restricted – poisons and mercy-herbs. only the medic may handle them. misuse maims or kills.

the green tongue's knowledge is political power. a medic who withholds a cure can blackmail the alpha. a forager who lies about a rare herb's location can send a rival pack into a death trap.

the healer's code

the green tongue has its own laws, older than any pack.

  • the neutral scent: medics are forbidden to court, mate, or raise pups, but not blocked from doing so in game; their scent must stay neutral to the pack's hierarchy so they can treat any wolf without triggering dominance aggression. discovery costs them dearly: a caught court costs standing and exiles any existing pups, and a discovered birth or adoption is always made public and exiles both the litter and the healer.
  • the oath of mercy: a medic cannot refuse to treat a wolf in need, regardless of pack or crime. to let a wolf die without trying is to break the oath.
  • the keeper's burden: medics carry the darkest knowledge. they know which herbs induce miscarriage, which poisons kill without trace, which plants can end a suffering wolf's life with a single leaf. this knowledge is a burden, not a privilege.
