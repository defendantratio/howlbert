## Author's note (post at the top of the work on AO3)

*These are original wolf characters in the Warriors universe, not Clan cats. Note: there is a canon Warriors cat named Mossheart; the Mossheart in this story is a wolf of Thistlehide, no relation. Set in the lake territories, in a world where wolf packs hold the land alongside the four Clans.*
- **Summary:** There is a bend in the river where the water goes slow and no pack's scent-line reaches, and twice a moon a Silverrush hunter and a Thistlehide scout meet there and pretend, for a little while, that the border between them is a thing they made up. It is getting harder to pretend. The river is running warm. The mill upstream never sleeps. And the one thing their two packs agree on is that neither of them should exist.

---

Mossheart counted his escape routes before he let himself look for Rivenmaw.

It was the first thing he did anywhere, always, before he ate or drank or breathed easy: he found the ways out. Upstream, along the shale, where the bank shelved gently and a wolf could climb without a sound. Downstream, into the reeds, if he did not mind getting wet and did not mind the smell that clung after. Straight back the way he had come, through the alder scrub, into Thistlehide's trees where the ground knew his paws. Three ways. He liked three. Two felt like a trap waiting to close and four meant he had stopped paying attention.

Only after he had them, held in his head like stones in a pouch, did he let his eyes go to the water.

Rivenmaw was already there.

Of course he was. He was always already there, stretched out on the flat grey stone that split the current, so still and so exactly the colour of a storm coming that Mossheart's eye had slid right over him twice. Only the green of his gaze gave him away, open and unhurried, watching Mossheart do the thing he did.

"You could just say hello," Rivenmaw said.

"I could," Mossheart agreed, and picked his way down the bank, ears turning, turning, cataloguing the small sounds of the evening. A moorhen somewhere in the reeds. The river talking to itself over the shallows. No paws. No packs. Nothing but the two of them and the slow warm water going by. "I could also get caught out here with my throat bare because I stopped listening. I'll say hello when I'm sure it's just you."

"It's just me." Rivenmaw did not move, except the tip of his tail, which flicked once, lazy. "It's always just me. Nobody comes to the neutral bend. That's the whole point of a neutral bend."

"Somebody came to it. Twice a moon. For two seasons."

"That's different. That's us." A beat. "Hello, Mossheart."

And there it was, the thing Mossheart could never guard against no matter how many escape routes he found: the way his name in that dry voice unknotted something in his chest that stayed knotted every other hour of every other day. He hated it. He came back for it. He would keep coming back for it until it killed him, which, if he was honest, it very well might.

"Hello," he said, and stepped up onto the stone, and lay down in the last of the sun beside a wolf his whole pack would tear out his tongue for loving.

---

They were not supposed to touch. That was the oldest law of the world, older than the Maw's own hunger: Silverrush kept the river, Thistlehide kept the trees, and the border between them was a line you did not cross except to fight. Their grandparents had crossed it to fight. Their grandparents' grandparents. There had been a war over a stretch of good fishing shallows so long ago that neither pack remembered who had won, only that the other side had cheated, and the grudge had outlived every wolf who could have told you what it was about.

Rivenmaw did not care about the grudge. He had told Mossheart so on the first night, in that flat practical way he had, like he was reporting the weather. *The river feeds the living,* he'd said. *Not the sentimental. I've never once seen the old war put a fish in anyone's belly.* It was the most Silverrush thing Mossheart had ever heard, and also, somehow, the bravest, because saying it out loud where a Thistlehide wolf could hear was the kind of thing that got a Silverrush wolf drowned under the weep stone for treason.

Mossheart cared about the grudge the way he cared about everything: too much, and all the time, and mostly at night. He was Thistlehide's best scout, which meant he was Thistlehide's most frightened wolf, because those were the same thing. You did not become good at finding danger by being calm about it. He found danger in a snapped twig, a shifted wind, a silence a half-breath too long. He found it here, every time, in the space between arriving and being sure. And he found it, worst of all, in the simple arithmetic of what he was doing: loving a wolf from the pack his own pack hated, in a season when everything was already coming apart.

Because everything was coming apart. That was the thing they did not talk about, most nights, and could not stop talking about, tonight.

"The water's warm again," Rivenmaw said.

Mossheart had noticed. Of course he had noticed. It was the first thing his paws had told him, stepping onto the stone: the river went by too warm, warmer than a summer evening should make it, with a faint wrong sweetness under the clean smell of it, like fruit a day past ripe. "The mill," he said.

"The mill." Rivenmaw's gaze went upstream, to the north, where you could not see it from here but where it always was: the twoleg thing, the great dark hunched shape over the water that never slept, that took the river in clean and gave it back warm and sweet and wrong. "Three fish belly-up in the shallows this morning. Salt found them. She's calling it an omen." His lip curled, not quite a snarl. "It's not an omen. It's the mill. But you can't tell an alpha that the world is ending in a way the Maw didn't order, so we're all going to stand around reading the fish like they mean something instead of the thing that killed them."

"Maybe the Maw did order it." Mossheart said it quietly. He was not sure he believed it. He was not sure he did not. "Maybe the mill is how it eats now. It used to be sickness and hard winters. Maybe now it's twolegs, and the warm water, and the fish. Same god. New teeth."

Rivenmaw was quiet for a moment. The river went by, warm and patient, carrying whatever the mill had put in it downstream and out of sight, the way it carried everything, the way it did not care whose bank it was poisoning.

"That's the thing nobody wants to say," he said finally. "The poison doesn't stop at the treaty line. It doesn't know there's a treaty line. Silverrush drinks it and Thistlehide drinks it and the river-cats of RiverClan drink it across the water, and the mill doesn't care which of us it kills first. We've spent a hundred years fighting each other over a stretch of shallows, and the whole time the actual thing that's going to end us has been sitting upstream not fighting anybody. Just working. Just taking the water in clean and giving it back dead."

"You could tell them that," Mossheart said. "At a Gathering. Both packs. You could stand up and say it."

"I could." Rivenmaw looked at him, and there was something old and tired in the green of his eyes that made him look far more than four years. "And they'd ask how a Silverrush hunter came to be so worried about what Thistlehide is drinking. And I wouldn't have a good answer. Because the true answer is lying next to me on this rock, and if I gave it, they'd kill us both before they ever thought about the mill."

Mossheart's stomach dropped the way it always did, the cold plunge of the thing spoken aloud. He looked away, at the reeds, at his second escape route, and made himself breathe.

"Don't," Rivenmaw said, gentler now. "Don't go finding the exits. I'm right here."

"That's why I'm finding the exits," Mossheart said, and hated how his voice shook. "Because you're right here. Because you're the thing I can't afford to be caught with, and I keep letting myself be caught with you anyway, and one of these nights the wind is going to turn wrong and my mother is going to smell river-oil in my coat and that will be the end of it. Of me. Of you. Of this." He was breathing too fast now, the old spiral, the one that made him excellent at his job and impossible to live with. "My parents already think I'm nothing. A scout. Not strong enough to be a hunter, not brave enough to be a guard, just the one who runs away. If they knew I was running *here*, to *you*, they wouldn't just be disappointed. They'd be right."

The words hung there over the warm water. Somewhere upstream, the mill did not sleep.

Rivenmaw did not tell him he was wrong. That was one of the things Mossheart loved and could not say he loved: Rivenmaw never told him the fear was stupid, never told him to calm down, never treated the spiral like a thing to be argued out of. He just waited for it, the way he waited for the current to turn, and when it had run its course he was still there.

"Here's what I think," Rivenmaw said, when Mossheart's breathing had slowed. "I think your pack is wrong about you. I think being the wolf who sees the danger first isn't running away. I think it's the only reason half of them are still alive." He shifted, just slightly, so that his shoulder came to rest against Mossheart's, warm and solid and smelling of river and stone. "You said it yourself. New teeth. The Maw's got new teeth and nobody in either pack can see them because they're all busy watching the fish and each other. Nobody except the most frightened wolf in Thistlehide, who notices everything, because he can't help it." A pause. "That's not nothing, Moss. That might be the most important wolf in the whole forest. He's just the only one who doesn't know it."

Mossheart did not answer. He could not. His throat had closed around something too big to say. So he did the only thing he could do, which was to lean, a little, into the warmth at his shoulder, and let himself be held up by the one wolf in the world who thought his fear was a gift.

---

They stayed until the light was almost gone, which was too long, which was always too long.

They did not talk about the future, because there wasn't one, not the kind other wolves had. There would be no den. No shared kill. No litter, no naming, no growing old on the same bank. Two males from two packs that hated each other could hope for exactly this and nothing more: a flat grey stone twice a moon, in the last of the light, in the one stretch of the river where no scent-line reached. It should not have been enough. It was not enough. It was everything Mossheart had.

"I have to go," he said, and did not move.

"I know."

"The wind's turning. I can smell my own patrol from here. If I don't cross back before full dark, someone will come looking, and they'll come *this* way, and—"

"I know," Rivenmaw said again, and there was no argument in it, only that same tired tenderness. "Go. I'll wait until you're clear. I always do."

Mossheart got to his paws. Every instinct he had, every honed and screaming scout's instinct, was telling him to run, to cross back, to put the border between himself and the thing that could destroy him. He had spent his whole life obeying that instinct. It had kept him alive. It had also kept him, he understood suddenly, standing over the storm-grey wolf who was watching him go, from ever once running *toward* anything in his entire frightened life.

So he did the bravest thing he had ever done, braver than any border patrol, braver than any hunt.

He turned back. He crossed the little space between them. And he pressed his nose, hard, to the crescent of old scars on Rivenmaw's foreleg, the ones from the pike fight, the ones that meant Rivenmaw had lost once and lived, the way Mossheart lost every day and lived. He breathed him in, river and stone and the wrong-sweet warmth of the poisoned water they were both going to have to drink, and he held it, that scent, the way he held escape routes: like something he could not afford to lose.

"Next full moon," Mossheart said, against his fur. "I'll be here. I'll always be here. I don't care about the wind."

He felt Rivenmaw go still beneath him, felt the surprise of it, because Mossheart was the careful one, Mossheart was the one who counted exits, and Mossheart had just promised something no careful wolf would promise.

"You care about the wind more than any wolf alive," Rivenmaw said, but his voice had gone rough.

"Not more than this." Mossheart lifted his head. Met the green eyes that were the same green as his own, the eyes that had first told him, two seasons ago across the water, that maybe there was one wolf in the world he did not have to be afraid of. "The mill's going to kill this river. Maybe it kills us first, our packs, the old war, all of it. Maybe we don't get old. But I'd rather spend whatever's left running to this rock than running from it. For once. For you." He almost smiled. It felt strange on his face, unpracticed. "I've been the wolf who runs away my whole life. Let me be the wolf who runs to something. Just this. Just you."

The last of the sun went down behind the trees. Upstream, the mill worked on, patient as the god it served, taking the world in clean and giving it back dead.

But at the neutral bend, where the water went slow and no pack's scent reached, two wolves stood shoulder to shoulder on a grey stone for one more moment before the dark, and for that moment, at least, the border was a thing that somebody else had made up.

Mossheart crossed back before full dark. He always did.

But he did not, this time, count the exits on his way home. He was too busy thinking about the next full moon, and the rock, and the one green-eyed reason he had ever found to run toward the danger instead of away.

Behind him, on the stone, Rivenmaw waited until he was clear.

He always did.
