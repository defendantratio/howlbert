## Author's note (post at the top of the work on AO3)

*These are original wolf characters in the Warriors universe, not Clan cats. Set in the lake territories, in a world where wolf packs hold the land alongside the four Clans.*
- **Summary:** For nine years the Rotmother of the swamp has read the hunger of a wounded god in her dreams, and she has never once been wrong. She predicted the red river. She predicted the missing chunk of the moon. She predicted the blind pup born two territories away. There is only one question the Maw has never answered for her, and she has come to the edge of its throat, old and tailless and tired, to ask it one last time: *how do I die?*

---

Murkvein had not been afraid of anything in five years, and she was not going to start now, at the lip of the Belly-Rip, with the whole marsh holding its breath to watch her go.

She was old. She let herself think the word plainly, the way she let herself think all true things, because a Rotmother who lied to herself did not stay a Rotmother long; she ended face-down in this same black water with a younger wolf's teeth in her neck. Murkvein knew that better than anyone. She had put three of them here herself, over the years, the ones who mistook her patience for weakness and learned, in their last wet moments, the difference. The Belly-Rip kept them all now. The swamp did not give back what it swallowed. That was the whole theology of the place, if you wanted to call it that: the world was a wounded god, and the god was hungry, and the marsh was the part of it that was still digesting.

She had come to the marsh's mouth to be digested. She simply wanted to know how first.

The Belly-Rip was a tear in the floor of the world, a place where the ground stopped being ground and became something the mind refused to hold. Black water, thick and slow, breathing up bubbles of swamp-gas that broke with a sound like a sleeper turning over. On a still night you could hear it, a low wet murmur under everything, and if you were the kind of wolf who believed, and Murkvein was the only kind of wolf she had ever wanted to be, you understood that the murmur was the god itself, talking in its sleep. She had spent her life learning to understand it.

She was very good at it. That was not pride; pride was another lie a Rotmother could not afford. It was simply true. Nine years ago she had told a pack in the middle of a succession war that the river to the west would run red before the next full moon, and it had, thick and wrong and stinking of the twolegs' iron, and the wolves who had laughed at her had stopped laughing. She had told them a piece of the moon would go missing, and one grey dawn a wolf had looked up and seen a bite taken clean out of it, a shadow crossing the pale face, and the marsh had howled. She had told them, two territories away, in the forest where the tree-cats and the tree-wolves kept their quarrel, that a pup would be born without sight, and it had been, and the Thistlehide wolves still whispered about the swamp-witch who had known.

She had the scars to show for every one of them. Ritual cuts, low on her belly, each a prophecy received and paid for, because the Maw did not give its dreams away free; you opened yourself to it, you let it in, and it left a mark going out. Her underside was a map of everything the god had ever told her. She was, in a real sense, a thing the Maw had written on.

There was only one question it had never answered.

She had been asking for a year. *How do I die?* Not whether, she was old, she was tailless, her lungs rattled like reeds in a wind, whether was a settled thing. But how. Every wolf in the marsh, in the whole of the lake territories, cat and wolf alike, died a death the Maw chose: rot-lung, or the twolegs' iron, or another wolf's teeth, or the slow starving of a hard winter. The god ate everyone eventually, and it ate them each in a particular way, and Murkvein, who had spent nine years reading the particular ways it ate other creatures, wanted, before the end, the courtesy of knowing her own.

The dreams had gone quiet. For a year, nothing. She had begun to wonder if the Maw had simply finished with her, used her up, written all it meant to write and set the pen down. It was a lonely thought. She had never told anyone she had it.

---

Dusk had followed her out to the Rip, of course. Her second, her whisper-jaw, the one wolf in the pack she trusted to stand at her back and the one wolf she trusted least to stay there once she was gone. That was fine. That was as it should be. A Rotmother who did not breed rivals bred a pack that died with her. She had watched Dusk sharpen over the years the way she herself had once sharpened, and she felt, looking at him now in the green marsh-dark, something that was almost tenderness and almost warning and was, in the end, simply recognition.

"You shouldn't be out here alone," Dusk said. He kept his distance, the proper distance, near enough to guard and far enough to run. Smart. She had taught him that.

"I'm not alone." Murkvein looked out over the black water, over the slow-breaking bubbles, over the throat of the god she had served her whole strange life. "The Maw is here. It's always here. You just can't hear it the way I do."

Dusk was quiet. He did not believe the way she believed; few did, truly, under all the fear and the ritual. He believed she was right, which was a different and lesser thing. He had seen her be right too many times to doubt her, but doubting a wolf's power and sharing her faith were not the same, and both of them knew it.

"Go back to the den," she told him. "This is between me and the god. If it takes me tonight, you'll know by morning, and you'll do what you've been circling for a season now. I won't hold it against you. I did the same to the one before me." She showed him her worn yellow teeth, not quite a snarl, not quite a smile. "Just make sure you're right about the timing. The Rip remembers everyone who was wrong about the timing."

He held her gaze a moment longer than was safe, which told her more about how the next few years would go than any dream could. Then he dipped his head, the exact correct depth, and melted back into the reeds, and Murkvein was alone with the throat of the world.

She lay down at the very edge of the black water. Her old bones complained. She let them. She put her chin on her forepaws, her pale green eyes open on the slow dark surface, and she asked, one last time, the only question she had left.

*How do I die?*

And the marsh breathed. And a bubble rose, and broke, with its sound like a sleeper turning. And the low wet murmur under everything went on, patient, ancient, digesting, and for a long time it said nothing she could understand, and she began, at last, to make her peace with getting no answer at all. Perhaps that was the answer. Perhaps a wolf who had spent her life demanding the god's secrets did not get the courtesy of her own. Perhaps the last prophecy was that there would be no last prophecy, and she would simply stop, one grey dawn, mid-breath, the way anyone stopped, and the Rip would take her like it took everyone, without ceremony, without a word.

She could live with that. She could, she found, lying at the lip of the god's throat with her whole ruined body aching, die with that.

And then the water spoke.

Not in words. It had never been words. It was a shift in the murmur, a change in the pitch of the swamp's long sleep, and it went into her the way all the dreams had gone into her, through the old cut-map of her belly, through the scarred places where the god had written before. She understood it the way she had understood the red river and the bitten moon and the blind pup: completely, and instantly, and without any possibility of being wrong.

The Maw did not tell her that she would drown, though she would. It did not tell her it would be tonight, though it would. It told her something stranger and simpler, and it was, she realized, the only mercy the god had ever offered her, the only gift, at the very end, in exchange for a lifetime of listening.

It told her: *you already know. You have always known. You came home to me on your own paws, before I ever had to come for you. No other wolf in the marsh will die the way you are about to, old one, chosen, unafraid, at the edge of my throat with your eyes open. That is the prophecy. That is how you die. You die like a wolf who was never once, in nine years, wrong. You die on purpose.*

Murkvein let out a breath she felt she had been holding for a year.

She was not afraid. She had told the truth about that, at least, at the very start. But there is a difference between not being afraid and being at peace, and she had not known, until the black water told her, how badly she had wanted the second one.

She rose, slow, on her brittle old legs. Behind her, in the reeds, she could feel Dusk had not gone far, would never have gone far, was watching even now, learning the last thing she had to teach him, which was how to end. Good. Let him watch. Let him carry it back: the Rotmother went to the Rip and the Maw answered her and she walked into the god's own throat with her head up and her eyes open, and she was not wrong about that either, because she had never once been wrong.

The marsh was very quiet. Somewhere far off, across the pine-dark where the tree-cats kept their marsh border, a night-bird called and went still. The moon, whole again now, unbitten, hung pale over the black water and laid a road of light straight across it, into the middle of the Rip, into the deep place where the swamp did its digesting and did not give back what it swallowed.

Murkvein followed the road of moonlight to the water's edge. She thought, briefly, without grief, of the three rivals she had put here, and of the Rotmother before her, and of her own mother who had died before she could name an heir, all of them down in the warm black patience of the god, none of them lost, only kept.

*To be remembered by the living is the only immortality,* the old wolves said, and Dusk would remember, and the pack would tell it for years: how she went.

She stepped into the water. It was warm, warmer than a night should make it, faintly salt, exactly as it had been when she was young and starving and hiding at this same edge, a year at the lip of the god's throat, learning to hear it. It was, she thought, as the black water closed cool and then warm and then nothing at all over her tailless back, a great deal like coming home.

The marsh breathed. A bubble rose, and broke, with a sound like a sleeper turning over.

And the Rotmother, who had never once been wrong, was right one last time.
